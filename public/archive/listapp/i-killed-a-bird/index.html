I KILLED A BIRD

Geoff Coffey
/ 14 December 2015

  • Once when I was 13 or 14 a bird came down our chimney.

  • It was in the cold fireplace behind the closed glass doors.

  • The fireplace had a gas starter. You could turn a knob and flames would light the logs.

  • My father is a smart man. He finished premed in 3 years and had a long career as a very successful anesthesiologist, department chief, and hospital administrator.

  • But he's not a smart man if you know what I mean.

  • I'll get a garbage bag and we can try to get him into it my brother said.

  • No said my dad. It will just escape and poop all over the house. We need to get it to fly back up the chimney.

  • I'm not sure a bird can fly *up* a chimney we said.

  • Of course it can. It flew down, right?

  • I'm not totally convinced it works that way.

  • Of course it does. We just need to scare it enough that it will fly away.

  • I tapped the glass. I rapped the glass. I banged the doors.

  • The bird paced nervously.

  • If we turn on the fire it will have to fly away.

  • (He is not a smart man.)

  • I'm not sure that will work.

  • Nonsense. He turned on the fire.

  • The bird noticed the fire right away. The bird possessed that singular primordial certainty given to all living things that fire was a threat.

  • The bird paced. It kept its distance. It began to breath more quickly.

  • I don't think it is going to fly out.

  • Nonsense.

  • The bird became delirious probably from a combination of heat and terror. It became unsteady on its feet. It stumbled, jumped back up, stumbled again, and fell on its side in the fire.

  • Oh my god.

  • My brother turned off the fire.

  • The bird was charred on one side of its body but it was still breathing and trying to muster the strength to stand.

  • My brother got a garbage bag. We carefully opened the doors. The bird did not flee. We picked it up with the help of the bag. It shook in our hands.

  • Oh god.

  • We took the bird outside and laid it on the cinder block edging of an in-progress garden project in the side yard. It felt like a scene from a movie come to life. I heard myself use the phrase put it out of its misery. I was crying.

  • I took a heavy round log from the woodpile.

  • I held the log above its head. My heart pounded. I was so terrified. I think I was most afraid of the sound I was sure this would make.

  • Swiftly with far more determination than I felt I crushed its head completely. It became entirely still.

  • It is a tender mercy of God that I heard no sound at all. No crunch. No cry. Just a thump and silence.

  • I didn't want to look. I turned my head, lifted the log, and returned it to the pile.

  • When I turned back my brother had wrapped the bird in the bag and was carrying it away.

  • I yelled at my dad. He didn't say anything. My mother told me to be respectful.

  • I closed the fireplace doors.

  • We never spoke of it again.
